However, ILI cases then declined in Week 50 to 1661 and further to 1621 by Week 51, 2020. This declining trend after Week 49 suggests a tapering growth in ILI occurrences leading into the next period.

2. A clear negative correlation between past and future occurrences is observed. While past ILI data peak at 1695 (Week 49, 2020), the subsequent declining trend (Weeks 50–51, 2020) aligns with the lower future ILI occurrence of 1042 (5 weeks later). The reduction in ILI activity during the latter period of the 5-week dataset is reflected in the future decrease.

3. Multiple summarized report factors correlate closely with the decline in future ILI occurrences:  
   - Outpatient visits for ILI consistently remained low and stagnant at 1.6% for five consecutive weeks (Weeks 47–51, 2020), remaining below the national baseline of 2.6%. This stability indicates no significant upward momentum in ILI activity.  
   - Laboratory-confirmed influenza activity stayed at historic lows, with positivity rates of 0.2% to 0.3% across Weeks 47–51, 2020, far below typical seasonal levels. The low circulation of influenza viruses contributed to limited ILI growth.  
   - Public health interventions, including widespread masking, social distancing, and other COVID-19 mitigation measures, likely suppressed both influenza virus transmission and ILI activity. The reports consistently highlighted these factors as contributors to the unusually low ILI levels.

In summary, the future ILI occurrences after 5 weeks (1042) are explained by a declining trend in ILI cases during Weeks 50–51, 2020, a stagnation in outpatient ILI visit percentages below the national baseline, minimal influenza virus detections, and the dampening effects of extensive public health interventions during the COVID-19 pandemic. These combined factors maintained suppressed levels of ILI activity, resulting in the observed decrease.
